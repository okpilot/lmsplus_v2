2
Gas Composition
30.1.1.1.1
3 min
Zoom into Gas Composition. Reveal pie chart. Walk through each gas.
Key Points
  • Nitrogen 78% — dominant gas, not oxygen
  • Oxygen 21% — breathing + combustion
  • N₂ + O₂ = 99% of dry air
  • Rest ~1%: Argon 0.93%, CO₂ 0.04%
  • DRY air figures — water vapour separate
  • Composition constant to ~60 km
Ask the class
  • N₂ 78% + O₂ 21% = what’s left?~1%. Reinforces dominance of N₂+O₂.
  • This is DRY air. What else might be in real air?Leads to water vapour.
Misconceptions
  • O₂ is the main gas → no, N₂. Memory: N before O, 78 before 21.
4 ECQB Qs. Distractors swap N₂/O₂ or replace N₂ with He/H₂.

Phases 3–4: Water Vapour & Layers
30.1.1 — Lesson Plan
3
Water Vapour
30.1.1.2.1
3 min
Stay in Gas Composition — point to amber water vapour card. Water vapour is invisible, variable, and creates all weather.
Key Points
  • Water vapour: 0–4% by volume, variable
  • Troposphere: ~75% of atmospheric mass, ~99% of water vapour
  • Only gas that changes state (gas ↔ liquid ↔ solid)
  • State changes = latent heat = drives weather
  • Water vapour is invisible — clouds are droplets/ice, not vapour
  • Warmer air holds more vapour → more intense weather
Ask the class
  • If water vapour is invisible, what are clouds?Liquid droplets or ice crystals.
  • Remove all water vapour — what happens to weather?No clouds, rain, fog. Essentially no weather.
Misconceptions
  • Clouds = water vapour → no, clouds = condensed droplets/ice.
3 ECQB Qs. Weather in troposphere (not tropopause!), 75% mass, 99% vapour.
Board: Gas Composition — amber water vapour card
4
Atmospheric Layers
30.1.1.1.2
4 min
Zoom to Atmospheric Layers. Point to temperature profile. Start from ground, walk upward. Troposphere gets most time.
Key Points
  • Layers defined by how temperature changes with height
  • Troposphere (to ~11 km) — we fly here, all weather here
  • Stratosphere (11–50 km) — stable, almost no weather
  • Tropopause = boundary (detail in Phase 7)
  • Mesosphere (50–80 km), Thermosphere (80 km+)
  • Boundaries named “-pause”: tropopause, stratopause, mesopause
  • Cessna 172 ceiling ~14,000 ft = ~3 km, tiny fraction of troposphere
Ask the class
  • Troposphere to ~36,000 ft. What flies that high?Airliners. Sense of scale. Don’t assume they know.
Misconceptions
  • Tropopause = a layer → no, it’s a boundary.
  • Weather everywhere → no, concentrated in troposphere.
1 ECQB Q: “The lowest layer is the...” → Troposphere.

Phases 5–6: Temperature, Pressure & Density
30.1.1 — Lesson Plan
5
Temperature and the ISA
30.1.1.1.3
3 min
Stay on temp profile diagram. Trace the line from surface up. Then zoom to ISA card.
Key Points
  • Troposphere: temp falls ~2 °C / 1,000 ft (ISA lapse rate: 1.98 °C)
  • ISA sea level: 15 °C, 1013.25 hPa
  • At tropopause (~36,000 ft): −56.5 °C
  • Lower stratosphere: constant −56.5 °C to ~FL 650
  • Upper stratosphere: temp rises (ozone absorbs UV)
  • ISA = theoretical model, real conditions vary
Ask the class
  • 15 °C at sea level, −2 °C per 1,000 ft. Temperature at 5,000 ft?15 − 10 = 5 °C. Simple arithmetic.
  • Does temperature keep falling forever above troposphere?No — stops at tropopause. Leads to next.
Misconceptions
  • Temp always decreases → no, stops at tropopause, rises in stratosphere.
ISA values (15 °C, 1013.25, lapse rate) appear across many Met questions.
Board: Temp profile (trace curve) → ISA card
6
Pressure, Density & Temperature
30.1.1.1.4
3 min
Zoom to Key Relationships. Introduce pressure and density simply. No aircraft performance references.
Key Points
  • Pressure = weight of air above. Higher up = less pressure.
  • Pressure halves every ~18,000 ft
  • Density = how tightly packed air is. Decreases with altitude.
  • Warm air less dense, cold air more dense
  • Humid air less dense than dry air (H₂O lighter than N₂/O₂)
  • Molecular weights: H₂O=18, N₂=28, O₂=32
Ask the class
  • Pressure = weight of air above. Climb higher — increase or decrease?Decrease. Straightforward.
  • Is humid air more or less dense than dry air?Less. Counterintuitive. Only ask after teaching it.
Cross-references (suggestions)
  • Later Met topics (30.3): pressure/density affect altimeters. Just basics now.
  • If students covered PoF (050): relate density to lift. Suggestion only.
No direct ECQB Qs here, but essential for later topics.

Phases 7–8: Tropopause & Above
30.1.1 — Lesson Plan
7
The Tropopause
30.1.1.2.2
4 min
Zoom to Tropopause section. Start with table, then pan to images. Most exam-heavy part of 30.1.1.
Key Points
  • Boundary between troposphere and stratosphere
  • Where temperature stops decreasing
  • Acts as a lid — weather trapped below
  • Higher at equator (16–18 km), lower at poles (8–10 km)
  • Higher in summer, lower in winter
  • ISA: 11 km (36,090 ft) = mid-latitudes
  • Warmer below → higher tropopause
  • Jet streams form near tropopause breaks
Ask the class
  • Equator hotter, poles colder. Tropopause higher where?Equator. Warmer → higher. Reason from what was taught.
  • If tropopause is a lid, what happens when it’s very high?Taller clouds. Tropical thunderstorms.
Misconceptions
  • Tropopause = stratosphere → no, tropopause is the boundary.
  • Height varies with water vapour → no, season + latitude (ECQB distractor).
4 ECQB Qs — heaviest topic. ECQB uses 18/6 km alongside textbook 16–18 / 8–10 km.
Board: Tropopause — table → globe images
8
Above the Tropopause
30.1.1.2.3
2 min
Pan back to temp profile. Point to flat section above tropopause. Brief context, not deep dive.
Key Points
  • ISA: constant −56.5 °C from tropopause to ~FL 650
  • Above FL 650: temp rises (ozone absorbs UV)
  • Stratosphere: stable, horizontal movement, almost no weather
  • Airlines cruise here for smooth air
Ask the class
  • Looking at the diagram — does temp keep falling above tropopause?No — constant, then rises. Read from diagram.
No direct ECQB Qs on this LO.

Phase 9: Conclusion
30.1.1 — Lesson Plan
9
Conclusion
1 min
Zoom back out to full board (press 0). Circle back to opening question. Deliver clear summary.
Summary — tie back to the opening
  • We started by asking what the atmosphere is made of. Now you know: 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen — and a small but crucial variable amount of water vapour that creates all our weather.
  • The atmosphere is structured in layers. As pilots, we live and fly in the troposphere — the lowest layer, where all the weather happens.
  • Temperature, pressure, and density all decrease as we climb. The ISA gives us standard reference values: 15 °C and 1013.25 hPa at sea level, dropping at about 2 °C per thousand feet.
  • The tropopause is not at a fixed height. Higher at the equator, lower at the poles, higher in summer, lower in winter.
  • These are the foundations. Everything from here — clouds, wind, pressure systems, weather charts — builds on what you learned today.
12 exam questions for this topic. Three traps: swapped N₂/O₂, tropopause vs stratosphere, tropopause height varies with season+latitude (not water vapour).
