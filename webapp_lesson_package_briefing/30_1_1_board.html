The Atmosphere

Composition, extent & vertical division
7 LOs 12 ECQB questions ~25 min
In this lesson you will learn
  • What the atmosphere is made of — and why the answer surprises most people
  • How the atmosphere is divided into layers, and which layer matters for pilots
  • What happens to temperature, pressure, and density as you climb
  • What the tropopause is, where it sits, and why it varies
  • Why water vapour — invisible and tiny in quantity — creates all our weather
Gas Composition LO 1.1.1 LO 1.2.1
Composition of dry air by volume
78% Nitrogen (N₂)
21% Oxygen (O₂)
0.93% Argon (Ar)
0.04% CO₂
0 – 4% Water vapour (variable — not in dry air figures)
Nitrogen and oxygen together make up 99% of dry air. Everything else is trace amounts.
These are dry air figures — water vapour is separate, variable (0–4%), and we cover it below.
Water vapour is the only atmospheric gas that changes state (gas ↔ liquid ↔ solid) — this is what creates all weather.
The troposphere holds ~75% of atmospheric mass and ~99% of all water vapour.

The atmosphere is divided into layers based on how temperature changes with height.
The troposphere is where we fly and where all weather occurs.
Temperature falls at ~2 °C / 1,000 ft in the troposphere. Above the tropopause, it stops falling.
The ISA is a theoretical model — real conditions vary, but these are the reference values for aviation.
Tropopause LO 1.2.2

Higher in summer than winter. Higher over warm air masses. Jet streams form near breaks in the tropopause.
Tropopause height variation by latitude
Tropopause folds and breaks — jet stream location
The tropopause is a boundary, not a layer — it separates the troposphere from the stratosphere.
It acts as a lid on weather — the stable air above prevents weather from rising further.
Warmer surface air → higher tropopause. Colder surface air → lower tropopause.
The tropopause is higher in summer and lower in winter.
Key Relationships LO 1.1.4
↑ Altitude → ↓ Pressure — halves every ~18,000 ft
↑ Altitude → ↓ Temperature — troposphere lapse rate
↑ Altitude → ↓ Density — less pressure, cooler air
↑ Temperature → ↓ Density — warm air is less dense
↑ Humidity → ↓ Density — H₂O lighter than N₂/O₂
Pressure is the weight of air above you — less air above means less pressure.
Pressure halves approximately every 18,000 ft — a useful rule to memorise.
Density is how tightly packed the air molecules are — it decreases with altitude, heat, and humidity.
Wrap-up
In this lesson you learned
  • The atmosphere is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen — not the other way around
  • Water vapour (0–4%) is the only gas that changes state — and it drives all weather
  • The troposphere is the lowest layer (surface to ~11 km) — where we fly, where weather happens
  • Temperature drops at ~2 °C per 1,000 ft. ISA sea level: 15 °C, 1013.25 hPa
  • Pressure halves every ~18,000 ft. Warm air is less dense. Humid air is less dense.
  • The tropopause is higher at the equator, lower at the poles, higher in summer, lower in winter
These are the foundations. Everything from here — clouds, wind, pressure systems, weather charts — builds on this.
